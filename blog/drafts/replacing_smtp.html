The case for replacing SMTP
  • Email
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • Google+

PGP is already a walled garden, and "easing into" large systemic shifts is historically unsuccessful. It's time to replace a 33-year-old standard with something relevant to the platforms and privacy concerns of today.

How I learned to stop worrying and love the bomb

The "Simple Mail Transfer Protocol" has unquestionably momentous inertia, but we shouldn't be so afraid to question a standard first written in 1982. Though deployment advancements like SMTP-over-TLS have made the internet mail infrastructure safer from outside observers, SMTP by itself is wholly incompatible with true end-to-end privacy. PGP-over-email has served well as an integrable private container, but since it requires existing public keys (and all the distribution infrastructure they entail), it already creates a walled garden of participants, which poses a significant barrier to entry. History has shown us that approaching broad infrastructure changes gradually is far more painful than abrupt departures; rather than continuing to support an awkward marriage between privacy protections and a protocol that wasn't meant to support them, we would be far better off with a purpose-built solution.

Platforms aren't going away

  • There will always be a need for platforms.
    • If you're a fortune 500 company, you're sure as shit not going to have an email server operating for each and every one of your employees.
    • Plenty of individuals lack the resources, time, willpower, knowledge, etc, to be deploying their own servers -- not to mention the security expertise to make them safe from botnets.
    • Economies of scale will always exist
    • Somewhere there's a balance between distributed and centralized

Platforms will always be a privacy risk

  • Platforms should never require trust
    • Trust is bad
    • 3 billion people online. Your trust will be betrayed.
    • We need protocols that allow us to use platforms without trusting them
    • This eliminates standalone SMTP
    • Historically, use PGP over SMTP

PGP is already a walled garden

  • In addition to incredibly severe usability problems, PGP is a walled garden
    • Recipients must already have a public key
    • Senders must

The failed union of PGP and SMTP

  • PGP robs email of its core feature: simplicity of use
    • Email "just works" -- you need to do exactly two things to send email: 1) have an account and 2) know someone's email address
    • Mailing lists
    • Scalable sending to arbitrary numbers of recipients
  • SMTP prevents PGP from fully protecting privacy
    • Unencrypted headers
    • Unencrypted metadata
