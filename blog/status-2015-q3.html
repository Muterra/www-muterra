The project's current status as of the close of Quarter 3, 2015. Effectively the beginning of everything.

As explained in the project introduction page, Ethyr has three major aspects: its protocol (Muse), its application (Ethyr itself), and its supporting company (Muterra). This section is a brief overview of their respective statuses as of the beginning of October 2015.

Muterra

Muterra, Inc is registered as a Delaware C Corporation, headquartered in Oakland, CA. It is currently a one-man shop (that's me, Nick Badger), though I have spoken with a friend about him possibly coming on board. That discussion is fairly early on and pending a bunch of (primarily financial) milestones on my end.

On 4 September I left my previous position to focus on this project full-time. The timing was right for a variety of reasons, but everything was very much in its infancy. Muterra.io had a (very amateurish) landing webpage, and though I would guess I had compiled between 100 and 200 pages of notes by this point, I had very little to show for it.

The decision to quit and work full-time on this project was admittedly an intimidating one, because I had/have effectively zero financial safety net (short of imploding my entire life and moving back in with my parents, which I do not consider an option). For reference, here are a bunch of financial details:

  • Personal burn rate ~$3000/month
    • $1200/mo rent
    • $550/mo student loans
    • $200/mo phone, utilities, etc
    • $350/mo groceries (I average 3k-4k calories per day; it gets expensive)
    • $75/mo transit
    • $150/mo home consumables (sponges, cleaning stuff, TP, etc)
    • ~$475 left for everything else
  • ~$7500 in savings after cashing final paycheck and after August bills
  • ~$5500 in reserve investments (goodbye, retirement account) post fees

So, with that said, with absolute zero receipts the financial timeline looks like this:

  1. 4 Sept: Last day of steady paycheck. $7500
  2. ~7 Oct: clear first bills cycle. $5200 (did better than expected!)
  3. ~7 Nov: clear second bills cycle. $2200 est.
  4. ~10 Nov: reserve pull #1. $4700 est.
  5. ~7 Dec: clear third bills cycle. $1700 est.
  6. ~10 Dec: reserve pull #2. $3700 est.
  7. ~15 Dec: drop-dead financial date. Start looking for a job.
  8. ~7 Jan: clear fourth bills cycle. $700 est.
  9. ~ 31 Jan: start burning credit, or life starts financial implosion.

The company side of things is much simpler to account for.

  • Corporate burn rate <$50/month
  • $350 in the bank

In the past month the financial situation has remained unchanged. For the company, the three primary developments have been:

  1. Update webpage to not be quite so amateurish, and create an independent site for Ethyr.
  2. Start talking about their existence.
  3. Square away some internal details (streamline records, clean up server pipeline, etc).

Muse

As of 4 September 2015, the Muse protocol was at a serviceable draft level of completion: far enough along that I was comfortable writing prototype implementation software, but still nowhere near version 1.0. I had already written some preliminary experimental code of previous protocol versions, and they showed promise.

Very little has changed with the protocol in the month since then. It is still severely under-documented, though expanding and updating existing documentation, leading up to a security review, is the next step.

At a very high level, the protocol is currently intended to convert a

  1. data transport network
  2. capable of insecure binary message transmission
  3. between individual physical (or virtual) network nodes

into a

  1. secure overlay network
  2. capable of authenticated, confidential, verifiably intact binary message transmission
  3. between abstract network participants.

The protocol operates at a fairly low level and defines a series of "encrypted information container" (EIC) file/message formats that support:

  • "Private by default" information
  • One-to-any communications
  • Asynchronous data persistence
  • Digital identity management
  • Information sharing management

Additionally, there are two work-in-progress Muse Overlay Standards proposals that outline:

  • A common public identity storage format
  • A common private identity key storage format

And two associated file formats:

  • zbg, an unencrypted binary structured object serialization format
  • harch, an unencrypted binary hyper-archive format

These are all, of course, subject to limitations and assumptions that will be outlined in future documents.

Ethyr

As of 4 September, Ethyr was only an idea and a bare domain. For all intents and purposes, it did not exist. Though it still isn't much closer to existing, that idea is starting to see the light of day, and I've made good progress (though it's felt too slow). The three highlights are:

  1. I have a much better sense of what I'd like to see for an alpha release
  2. Ethyr has a landing page and email list
  3. Ethyr now has a blog

That doesn't sound like much, and in the grand scheme of things, it isn't. However, since I'm as short-staffed as I am, those last two points in particular entailed a whole lot of work:

  • Get AWS hosting infrastructure up
    • Shared with Muterra.io
    • It takes me only 5-10 minutes to update and deploy new static servers
    • Auto-scaling server infrastructure to demand
  • Catch up to the past 10 years of web development
    • Re-familiarize with HTML
    • Re-familiarize with CSS
    • Re-familiarize with JS, including "huh, jquery looks neat"
    • Learn how to do anything responsive
    • Get comfortable with static site generation (using Pelican, in my case)
    • Get frustrated by existing blog template design
    • Spend too long rolling your own
    • Make a bunch of mistakes and learn from them
    • Start boxing with the CSS box model
  • Get a better artistic feel for web design
    • Make a lot of mistakes and learn from them
    • Do a lot of "comparison shopping"
    • Small amount of asset creation
  • Actually announce Ethyr and then fight the few resulting fires on the landing page
  • Integrate with external tools like Mailchimp
  • Etc

I'm happy to report that the learning curves have mostly subsided, and I can quite easily add new posts to a blog that I don't totally hate. There's definitely room for improvement, but given my limited supply of man-hours, I've shifted my priorities elsewhere.
